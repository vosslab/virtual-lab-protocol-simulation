convert units of measurements, make solutions and make dilutions.

### Introduction to solutions

When talking about solutions, it is important to remember that a
***solute*** is dissolved in a ***solvent*** to create a ***solution***.
In any biology lab, 99.99% of the time the solvent is distilled water
(dH<sub>2</sub>O) or a pre-existing solution of solutes in water.

$$\text{solute}{(\mathit{solid})}{+}\text{solvent}{(\mathit{water})}{=}\text{solution}$$

One of the most important aspects of carrying out your experiments is
the quality of your reagents. Many reagents are used often and are kept
as stock solutions at high concentrations. These reagents can be salt
solutions, buffers, detergents and storage solutions. Salts and buffers
are used to ensure enzyme activity and mimic the cellular ionic and pH
environment. Detergents help in the isolation of proteins or other
cellular components usually by denaturing proteins, destroying the cell
wall or membrane or making components that are insoluble in water more
soluble. Storage solutions allow cells and cell material to be frozen
for long term storage when not immediately needed and allows the cells
to live after thawing.

Solutions are stored at high concentrations as molar solutions or as a
weight per volume sometimes given as a percentage. Solutions are often
made as "stocks", meaning that they are stored at a much higher
concentration than they are used. This allows the user to save time in
preparing "working" solutions, or to mix multiple solutions in the
proper amount and dilute them with each other and with water.

### Description of Concentration

Concentration is defined as the abundance (in various forms) of a solute
divided by the total volume of a solution. There are five forms of
concentrations: mass concentration, percent mass concentration, molar
concentration, number concentration, and volume concentration.

**Mass concentration** is the amount of mass per unit volume. Typically,
biology this is given in grams per milliliter or *g/mL*. For example a
protein sample concentration may be given as 10 mg/mL (or 1% (w/v) as
described below).

**Percent mass concentration** is the amount of mass per unit volume
written as a percentage. Some solutions are stored as percent weight per
volume (w/v). It is a variation on the above measure. For this the %
equals grams per 100 mL. A 10% solution would be 10 g in 100 mL.
However, what if you only want 10 mL of solution? Now you need to
convert g/100 mL to g/10 :. A good strategy is to calculate the grams
per mL and multiply by the total desired volume. The conversion is from
g/mL to a percentage, using the formula:

$$0.01\frac{\text{g}}{\text{mL}}{=}10.0\frac{\text{mg}}{\text{mL}}{=}1.0\frac{\text{g}}{100\text{mL}}{=}1.0\text{\%}{({\text{w}/\text{v}})}$$

Therefore, if you have a 30% (w/v) solution of sucrose, it means you
have a mass concentration of 0.3 g/mL or 300 mg/mL.

*Note: *the "w*/v*" stands for weight divided by volume, unlike the
"v/v" used below. Several times the description of the percent is left
out and you must determine whether it means volume per volume or weight
per volume.

**Molar concentration** is the number of moles per unit volume. This
commonly the preferred method for providing a concentration and is given
in the units mol per Liter or Molar (M). For example, the salt
concentration of buffer may be given as 100 mM.

**Number concentration** is the number of objects per unit volume. You
could say that there are 24 students per lab as a form of number
concentration. Number concentration is not normally used in
molecular biology, because there are typically too many objects and it
moles. But it is commonly used in cellular biology, as a concentration
of cells, for example, 1.2 x 10<sup>6</sup> cells/mL. This is because
there are numerous cells in solution, but not enough to be specified in
moles.

**Volume concentration** is used when dissolving one volume into
another. Volume concentration is given as a percentage of the total
volume. For example, if you add 300 mL of ethanol and fill with water
until you get to exactly 1000 mL, you would have a *30% (v/v) ethanol
solution*. Note: the "v*/v*" stands for volume divided by volume.

### Making solutions

Salts typically come in solid form, so most often need to convert grams
measured on a balance to moles. A mole of a compound is equal in grams
to the formula weight of the compound. This number can be calculated
from a periodic table or, often, the bottle will list the formula
weight.

$$\frac{\text{mass in grams}}{\text{formula weight}}{=}\text{number of moles}$$

*Note:* some compounds contain water molecules and their formula weight
will differ from the molecular weight of the elements alone, it is
therefore a good idea to check the label of the compound to find out if
it is anhydrous (without water).

Example 1: Make a 1 M solution of NaCl (sodium chloride) in 1 Liter (L)
of solution.

First calculate the amount of salt you will need in grams (g): Looking
at a periodic table, NaCl has a formula weight of 22.99 g per mole (Na)
plus 35.45 g per mole (Cl) which equals a total of *58.44 g per mole*.
We want exactly 1 mole of NaCl, so we need to weigh out 58.4 grams.

Example 2: make a 200 mM (=200 mmol/L) solution of NaCl in 500 mL of
solution.

Now you must calculate the grams of NaCl required for only half the
amount of liquid. For these types of calculations it is easiest to set
up a units table. A units table is where all the numbers along with
their units are placed in a table such that the units will cancel out,
i.e., whenever a unit is on the top of the table a corresponding unit
is placed on the bottom. The only units remaining are the units of your
final answer. For example, in example problem \#1, we would use the
following units table:

$$\begin{pmatrix}
{1\text{mol}} & \mid & {58.44\text{g}} & \mid & {1\text{L}} \\
{1\text{L}} & \mid & {1\text{mol}} & \mid &
\end{pmatrix}{=}58.4\text{g}$$

In this table, all the units cancel out except grams. We then multiply
the top numbers and divide by the bottom numbers to get 1 \* 58.44 \* 1
/ (1 \* 1) = 58.4 grams, the answer to example problem \#1. Now to
tackle, the harder problem, since example \#2 has more units, we need to
convert millimolar (mM) into molar (M) and milliliters (mL) into
liters (L).

$$\begin{pmatrix}
{200\text{mmol}} & \mid & {1\text{mol}} & \mid & {58.44\text{g}} & \mid & {500\text{mL}} & \mid & {1\text{L}} \\
{1\text{L}} & \mid & {1000\text{mmol}} & \mid & {1\text{mol}} & \mid & & \mid & {1000\text{mL}}
\end{pmatrix}{=}5.8\text{g}$$

Using the above table, you should see that all the units cancel out
except grams (the units you are expecting to get out). Now it is just a
matter of multiplying the top numbers and dividing by the bottom
numbers. 200 \* 1 \* 58.44 \* 500 / (1 \* 1000 \* 1 \* 1000) =
5.8 grams. This makes sense since, we are making half the volume at one
fifth the concentration, it is not surprising to add one tenth the salt.

### Dissolving the Solids

Most balances that weigh 10s to 100s of grams are usually accurate to
only the first decimal point. It varies with the scale and is usually
listed on the balance. Therefore, you may need to round off your
calculations to the correct decimal point before you go to weigh your
solid. Next weigh your weigh paper and tare the instrument (make it
equal zero with the paper on it).

Add the amount of compound until you reach the total weight. Next, add
the solid slowly to distilled water (dH<sub>2</sub>O) that is stirring.
*Do not start with the total volume of water because adding the solid
will increase the volume.* Obtain about 75 to 80% of the final volume of
water that you need (depending on how much material you plan to add).

After the solid dissolves, add distilled water (dH<sub>2</sub>O) to the
final volume. If using with cells, place the solution in an autoclave
flask to destroy any living cells. For chromatography, it sometimes
requires to *degas* the solution (*i.e.*, remove any dissolved gases in
the liquid). In order to degas a solution, place the solution under
vacuum, such as an aspirator attached to a sink faucet.

## Dilutions: Concepts and Calculations

### Dilution Goals:

1.  Understand how to make a dilution using c<sub>1 </sub>x
    V<sub>1</sub> = c<sub>2 </sub>x V<sub>2</sub>
2.  Understand concentration factor and dilution factor
3.  Understand when it is necessary to make a serial dilution and how to
    do one
4.  Be able to combine several stock solutions in order to make a
    "working" solution.

### Introduction

When talking about dilutions, it is important to remember that an
***aliquot*** (a portion of a total amount of a solution) is diluted in
a ***diluent*** to create a ***dilution***. In any biology lab, most of
the time the diluent is distilled water (dH<sub>2</sub>O) or another
bulk solution, such as cell media.

$$\text{aliquot}{(\mathit{sample})}{+}\text{diluent}{(\mathit{water})}{=}\text{dilution}$$

Many students appear to panic when they must dilute something, yet the
mathematics involve nothing worse than the simplest algebra. That
problem can be overcome by practicing in advance of the need. Even with
practice, though, you may find dilution problems confusing unless you
very clearly define your objectives. We will give you a useful formula
for making dilutions, one that you may have seen before. The formula is
worse than useless, though, if you don't use it properly.

### Establish a frame of reference

Suppose you have a starting solution with volume V<sub>1</sub> and
concentration c<sub>1</sub>. What is the total amount of solute in your
solution?

Answer: c<sub>1 </sub>x V<sub>1</sub>

For example, let's say we have a volume of 0.2 L and the concentration
is 50 g/L.

c<sub>1 </sub>x V<sub>1</sub> = 50 g/L x 0.2 L = 10 grams

Now suppose that you dilute that solution with water - the whole thing -
to some larger, predetermined volume (V<sub>2</sub>). What mass of
solute is present in the new, diluted solution?

If you said 10 grams, you get the gold star. But wait a minute, c<sub>1
</sub>x V<sub>1</sub> = 10 grams, and the new solution has a different
volume, V<sub>2</sub>. The same amount of solute is present in the new
solution as was in the original solution, so the following relationship
must hold:

$$\text{c}_{1}{\times}\text{V}_{1}{=}\text{c}_{2}{\times}\text{V}_{2}$$

where c<sub>2</sub> = concentration of the new solution.

Example 3. Dilute the previous solution (0.2 L at 50 g/L) to 2.0 L.

What is the concentration of the new solution? We must solve for
c<sub>2</sub>, of course. c<sub>1 </sub>x V<sub>1</sub> = c<sub>2
</sub>x V<sub>2</sub> = 10 grams. We know that V<sub>2</sub> = 2 L, so
now we have

c<sub>2 </sub>x (2 L) = 10 g

Solve for c<sub>2</sub> to obtain *5 g/L*.

Determining what you already know and putting the information into the
equation c<sub>1 </sub>x V<sub>1</sub> = c<sub>2 </sub>x V<sub>2</sub>
establishes the relationship that you need in order to solve dilution
problems.

Second, we need to determine the amount of diluent (V<sub>*d*</sub>) to
be added. It is simply the final dilution value (V<sub>2</sub>) minus
the aliquot volume (V<sub>1</sub>) or:

$$\text{V}_{d}{=}\text{V}_{\text{dilution}}-\text{V}_{\text{aliquot}}{=}\text{V}_{2}-\text{V}_{1}$$

So, in the above example, the diluent volume (V<sub>*d*</sub>) =
V<sub>2</sub> - V<sub>1</sub> = 2 L - 0.2 L = *1.8 L*.

### Determine the objective

What do you want to do? Or, more realistically, what does the instructor
want you to do? Two types of dilution problems are quite common in
biology and biochemistry labs.

1.  Dilute a known volume (V<sub>1</sub>) of known concentration
    (c<sub>1</sub>) to a desired final concentration (c<sub>2</sub>).
2.  Dilute a known concentration (c<sub>1</sub>) to a desired final
    concentration (c<sub>2</sub>) and volume (V<sub>2</sub>).

The second type of problem really throws people off! Let's start with
the first one, though. You know V<sub>1</sub>, c<sub>1</sub>, and
c<sub>2</sub> is predetermined, so we must solve for V<sub>2</sub>, the
final volume to which to dilute the solution. This one is easy, since
you keep the amount of solute the same and only have to change one
factor.

Example 4. Dilute the previous solution (0.2 L at 50 g/L) to 10 g/L.

We have 0.2 L of our 50 g/L solution and we want a final concentration
of 10 g/L. To what volume do we need to dilute our solution?

c<sub>1 </sub>x V<sub>1</sub> = c<sub>2 </sub>x V<sub>2</sub>

50 x 0.2 = 10 x V<sub>2</sub>

V<sub>2</sub> = 50 x 0.2 / 10 = 1 L

So, we need to dilute our solution to 1 L and to obtain a final
concentration of 10 g/L. Likewise, the diluent volume (V<sub>*d*</sub>)
= V<sub>2</sub> - V<sub>1</sub> = 1 L - 0.2 L = 0*.8 L*.

Now for the second type problem. You know c<sub>1</sub>, the starting
concentration. You know both desired final volume V<sub>2</sub> and
desired final concentration c<sub>2</sub>. There is one undetermined
variable left, namely V<sub>1</sub>. V<sub>1</sub> is the volume of
original solution or aliquot that you will dilute using diluent to the
desired final volume and concentration.

Example 5. Prepare a 100 mL of sucrose solution at a concentration
2.5 g/L using a 47 g/L stock solution.

Since you know the starting concentration of sucrose and you know both
the final concentration and volume of solution that you want, all you
need to find out is what volume of starting solution or aliquot
(V<sub>1</sub>) to use.

c<sub>1 </sub>x V<sub>1</sub> = c<sub>2 </sub>x V<sub>2</sub>

47 x V<sub>1</sub> = 2.5 x 100

V<sub>1</sub> = 2.5 x 100 / 47 = 5.3 mL

Notice the final answer is in milliliters (mL) not liters (L), that is
because we plugged milliliters in the equation. It is important to make
sure all of your units cancel when using these equations. Likewise, the
diluent volume (V<sub>*d*</sub>) = V<sub>2</sub> - V<sub>1</sub> =
100 mL - 5.3 mL = *94.7 mL* of water.

Example 6. Prepare a 100 mL of NaCl solution at a concentration 25 mM
using a 2 M stock solution.

Once again, we know the starting concentration (c<sub>1</sub>) and both
the final concentration (c<sub>2</sub>) and volume (V<sub>2</sub>).

c<sub>1 </sub>x V<sub>1</sub> = c<sub>2 </sub>x V<sub>2</sub>

2 x V<sub>1</sub> = 25 x 100

V<sub>1</sub> = 25 x 100 / 2 = 1250 mL

So, just by putting in the numbers it says we need to dilute a 1250 mL
aliquot to form 100 mL of solution. That is *impossible*! What did we do
wrong? Well, we did not make sure our units cancelled out. If we did it
properly, we would convert 2 M into 2000 mM (or 25 mM into 0.025 M) and
it would look like this:

c<sub>1 </sub>x V<sub>1</sub> = c<sub>2 </sub>x V<sub>2</sub>

2000 x V<sub>1</sub> = 25 x 100

V<sub>1</sub> = 25 x 100 / 2000 = 1.25 mL

This is a much more reasonable answer. It now says we need to dilute a
1.25 mL aliquot to form 100 mL of solution. As before, the diluent
volume (V<sub>*d*</sub>) = V<sub>2</sub> - V<sub>1</sub> = 100 mL -
1.25 mL = *98.75 mL* of water.

It is critical that you report units for concentrations, volumes, and
amounts, and when you make calculations for dilutions you must not mix
up the units. For example, it doesn't work to write:

V<sub>1</sub> (160 mL) x c<sub>1</sub> (160 mg/L) = V<sub>2</sub>
(unknown) x c<sub>2</sub> (3 g/L)

However, because concentration represents a proportional relationship,
you can select from a variety of units. For example, 1 mg/mL is the same
as 1 g/L, 1 &micro;g/&micro;L, 1 ng/nL, *etc*. It is also the same as 1000 mg/L, but
why would we write it that way? Select units that simplify your
expressions.

### Concentration and dilution factors

Because solutions in science are often much more concentrated than are
desired or can be managed for a given protocol, it is frequently
necessary to dilute these solutions to a desired level. This requires a
working knowledge of the principles of diluting, dilution factors,
concentration factors and the calculations involved. High dilutions are
usually expressed exponentially, *i.e.*, a solution which has been
diluted a million fold is termed a 10<sup>6</sup> dilution (DF), or is
10<sup>-6</sup> concentration (CF).

Sometimes instead of telling you the desired volume or concentration of
a solution a dilution factor (DF) is provided. The dilution factor (DF)
is defined as:

$$\text{DF}{=}\frac{\text{final volume}}{\text{aliquot volume}}{=}\frac{\text{V}_{2}}{\text{V}_{1}}$$

or based on c<sub>1 </sub>x V<sub>1</sub> = c<sub>2 </sub>x
V<sub>2</sub>, we convert it into terms of concentration (notice the
numbers are flipped):

$$\text{DF}{=}\frac{\text{aliquot concentration}}{\text{final concentration}}{=}\frac{\text{c}_{1}}{\text{c}_{2}}$$

Alternatively, the concentration factor (CF) can be provided, it is
simply the inverse of the dilution factor (DF)

$$\text{CF}{=}\frac{1}{\text{DF}}{=}\frac{\text{aliquot volume}}{\text{final volume}}{=}\frac{\text{V}_{1}}{\text{V}_{2}}$$

Typically dilutions are written as a "1:50 dilution." In this example,
50 is the dilution factor (DF) and 1/50 = 0.02 is the concentration
factor (CF). *As a rule of thumb, the dilution factor (DF) will be
greater than 1 and the concentration factor (CF) will always be less
than 1.*

In order to calculate the volume of the aliquot, it can be determined in
two ways:

- aliquot volume = final volume / dilution factor (DF)
- aliquot volume = final volume \* concentration factor (CF)

Example 7. How would you prepare 20 mL of a 1:50 dilution?

Determine required aliquot by dividing final volume by dilution factor:
20 mL/50 = 0.4 mL sample. Subtract the aliquot volume from the final
volume:  20 mL - 0.4 mL = 19.6 mL diluent. Measure out 19.6 mL diluent,
add 0.4 mL sample to it, mix thoroughly

### Serial Dilutions

A serial dilution is the **stepwise** dilution of a substance in
solution. Serial dilutions are used to accurately create highly diluted
solutions from a highly concentrated stock solution. Many areas of
science use serial dilutions in the preparations for different
experiments. Most commonly, serial dilutions are used to reduce the
concentration of cells in a sample.

**Figure 1:** Diagram of serial dilutions

This technique involves the removal of a small amount of an original
solution to another container which is then brought up to the original
volume using the required buffer or water.

This exercise is presented as an aid for students to improve their
skills and more quickly understand the particular application for which
serial dilutions are a tool. Serial dilutions are often used in
microbiology, biotechnology, and in chemistry classes, to name just a
few. Therefore a clear and concise approach to the learning of this very
important concept is essential.

Serial dilutions are *usually* made in increments of 1000, 100 or 10.
The concentration of the original solution and the desired concentration
will determine how great the dilutions need to be and how many dilutions
are required. Important also is the total volume of solution needed. If
only small quantities of solutions are needed then greater numbers of
dilutions are necessary.

The most common examples deal with concentration of cells or organisms,
or the concentration of a solute. The approximate concentration must be
known at the start of the experiment before the appropriate number and
amount of dilutions can be made. In order to accurately arrive at the
desired concentration, use serial dilutions, instead of making one big
dilution. Adding 1 &micro;L of sample to 999 mL of water (for a
1:10<sup>6</sup> dilution) never makes sense. Using a serial dilution
provides two additional advantages over one large dilution:

1.  *Saves materials.* Allows for small aliquots to be diluted instead
    of unnecessarily large quantities of materials.
2.  *Increases accuracy.* If you are off only slightly on the aliquot
    volume the concentration goes way off.

Example 8: Let us demonstrate the second property on a 1:10<sup>6</sup>
dilution, using three steps of 1:100 dilutions versus one big step.

*One big step method.* In the one big step method, we are going to
dilute the sample one time, doing a 1:10<sup>6</sup> dilution to get a
final volume of 10 L. This is easy,

- *Step 1:* aliquot 10 &micro;L of sample to 9999.99 mL (~10 L!) of water to
  form final dilution. (DF = 10<sup>6</sup>)

The 1:10<sup>6</sup> dilution is done. Do we really need 10 L of sample?
Where are we going to get all this distilled water?

*Three step method.* In the three step method, we are going to dilute
the sample three times, doing a 1:100 dilution each time to get a final
volume of 100 L.

- *Step 1:* aliquot 1 mL of sample to 99 mL of water to form
  dilution #1. (DF = 100)
- *Step 2:* aliquot 1 mL of dilution #1 to 99 mL of water to form
  dilution #2. (DF = 100)
- *Step 3:* aliquot 1 mL of dilution #2 to 99 mL of water to form final
  dilution. (DF = 100)

Now, let's say our pipet was off by 1 uL. This means that when we want 10 &micro;L
we get 9 &micro;L and when we want 1 mL, we get 0.999 mL. What are the
adjusted dilution factors?

- *One big step method.*** **Dilution factor (DF) = 10 L/9 &micro;L = (1 x
  10<sup>7</sup> &micro;L)/9 &micro;L = *1.11 x 10*<sup>*6*</sup>. An error of 11%.
- *Three step method.*** **Dilution factor (DF) = (100 mL/0.999 mL) \*
  (100 mL/0.999 mL) \* (100 mL/0.999 mL) = (100 mL/0.999 mL)<sup>3</sup>
  = *1.003 x 10*<sup>*6*</sup>. An error of 0.3%.

**Example 9:** If you have 1 mL of your original solution, and you
remove a 10 µL aliquot and place it in a tube containing 990 µL of
diluent making a 1:100 dilution. If the original solution contained 5 x
10<sup>8</sup> cells/mL, we now have a concentration of 5 x
10<sup>6</sup> cells/mL, because we have simply divided our
concentration by 100. Now, if we want to dilute this by a factor of
1:1000, we must remove 1 µL of the second solution and place it in a
tube containing 999 µL of media. We have now diluted our secondary
concentration by 1000, and would then divide our concentration by 1000
to yield a 5 x 10<sup>3 </sup>cells/mL.

**Example 10:** Using a previous diluted sample at DF = 100, create a new
dilution with a final dilution of DF = 180 and a total volume of 720 &micro;L.
What volume of aliquot in microliters (&micro;L) do you add to distilled water

Calculate the effective dilution factor. DF = DF2/DF1 = 180/100 = 1.8.
So the effective dilution factor is 1.8.

aliquot volume = final volume / dilution factor (DF) = 720 &micro;L / 1.8 =
